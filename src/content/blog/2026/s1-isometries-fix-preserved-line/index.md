This is a small post about one-parameter subgroups of isometries,
and facts that I keep needing.

The first fact:

> If $S^1$ acts by isometries and preserves a one-dimensional
> submanifold $L$, then the action fixes $L$ pointwise.

Spoiler: after writing things out a bit I realized that the claim as
stated is false if $L$ is a circle, so I really should have said
"non-compact one-dimensional submanifold."

---

## The vector field of the action

Look at the vector field $X$ generated by the action. Since the
action is by isometries, $X$ is a Killing field, and its flow is the
$S^1$-action itself.

Preservation of $L$ says the flow sends $L$ into $L$, so $X$ is
tangent to $L$ at every point of $L$. Restricting, $X|_L$ is a
vector field on the one-dimensional manifold $L$.

Two cases.

**$X$ vanishes on all of $L$.** The flow fixes $L$ pointwise. Done.

**$X$ is nonzero somewhere on $L$.** Pick $p \in L$ with $X(p) \neq 0$.
The flow line through $p$ stays in $L$, and it's periodic: the flow
comes from an $S^1$-action, so $p$ returns to itself after time $2\pi$.

But $L$ is one-dimensional. The flow leaves $p$ in the direction
$X(p)$. To return, the flow line would have to come back to $p$, and
on a line, the only way back is to approach $p$ from the side it left
from. That means arriving at $p$ in the direction $-X(p)$. But the
velocity at $p$ is $X(p)$, by definition; $X$ is a single-valued
vector field and doesn't get to point both ways at the same point.

So the only way out is a circle. There, the flow line can return to
$p$ from the *other* side, in the same direction $X(p)$, by going all
the way around. That's exactly how a circle rotates by itself, and
it's why the claim needs the non-compactness assumption.

---

## Why I wrote this down

This came up when Steve and I were trying to come up with a working
definition of what a surface of revolution in a manifold should be.
We wanted to say that a surface of revolution is a surface that admits
an $S^1$ action by isometries preserving a "line" (the axis of
revolution).

The fact above is what makes the definition behave: it's enough to
say the action *preserves* a line, not that it *fixes* one.
Preservation forces fixing, once the axis is non-compact.
