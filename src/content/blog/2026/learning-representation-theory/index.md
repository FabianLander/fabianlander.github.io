Steve has been teaching me representation theory — on walks, and at park benches with pen and paper. This is a recap of what I've understood so far, and what I haven't.

We came to it from two unrelated questions, both about configuration spaces. One is the configuration space of a water molecule. The other is the configuration space of a mechanical linkage Steve and his collaborators study (see his post on [sphere linkages](https://stevejtrettel.site/blog/2026/sphere-linkages-realistic/)). I won't say much about either here — this post is about the representation theory itself, of finite or compact groups.

## What a representation is

I'll only talk about *linear* representation theory. A linear representation is a homomorphism

$$
\rho \colon G \to \mathrm{Aut}(V)
$$

from a group $G$ into the automorphisms of some vector space $V$. The vector space can be infinite-dimensional. The group will eventually be finite or compact for everything I want to say next, but the definition itself doesn't care.

In categorical language, the objects of the category of linear representations are triples: a group $G$, a vector space $V$, and a homomorphism $\rho \colon G \to \mathrm{Aut}(V)$.

A notation thing that confuses me: a lot of people call the representation simply $V$, dropping $\rho$ and $G$ from the name entirely. I find this hard to read — "the representation $V$" sounds like the vector space *is* the representation, when really the data is the action of $G$ on $V$. A few examples in the wild:

- Schedler's [Imperial lecture notes](https://www.imperial.ac.uk/PWP/document/lecture-notes-35.pdf) and Haiman's [Berkeley notes](https://www2.math.upenn.edu/~zvihr/courses/RTNotes.pdf) both slip between $(V, \rho)$ and just $V$.
- Diaconis' [Chicago notes](http://www.math.uchicago.edu/~margalit/repthy/Diaconis-groupsreps1.pdf) write things like $V = W \oplus W^\circ$ with $V$ standing in for the whole representation.

I'll do the opposite and call the representation $\rho$. This is also standard — character theorists write $\chi_\rho$ for the character of $\rho$, automorphic forms people say "the representation $\pi$," and physicists routinely name a rep by its homomorphism. So when I write "let $\rho$ be a representation of $G$ on $V$," $\rho$ is the rep and $V$ is the underlying vector space.

## Invariant subspaces and irreducibility

A subspace $W \subseteq V$ is **invariant** under $\rho$ if $\rho(g) W \subseteq W$ for every $g \in G$. Equivalently, $\rho$ restricts to a representation of $G$ on $W$.

$\{0\}$ and $V$ are invariant under any $\rho$ — for free. The representation $\rho$ is **irreducible** if those are the only invariant subspaces. Otherwise it's reducible: there is a smaller, nonzero subspace of $V$ that $G$ acts on without ever mixing it into the rest.

Maschke's theorem says that for finite $G$, irreducibility is the only obstruction to decomposing — every reducible $\rho$ splits, all the way down to irreducible pieces.

## The goal

What I want to understand is one of the main decomposition theorems — and at least one example of why it's so useful. The theorem is Maschke's:

> Let $G$ be a finite group and $\rho$ a representation of $G$ on a vector space $V$ over a field whose characteristic does not divide $|G|$ (e.g. $\mathbb{C}$). Then $\rho$ decomposes as a direct sum of irreducible representations.

$V$ does not need to be finite-dimensional. The averaging trick that proves the theorem works regardless. For finite $G$, every irreducible representation is itself finite-dimensional, so even when $V$ is infinite-dimensional, $\rho$ breaks into a (possibly infinite) direct sum of finite-dimensional irreducible pieces.

For compact groups there's an analogous statement (Peter–Weyl), but I'll stick with the finite case here.

## Easy examples

**Rotation of $\mathbb{R}^2$ by a fixed angle $\theta$.** Let $G = \langle R_\theta \rangle$ be the cyclic group generated by rotation by $\theta$, acting on $\mathbb{R}^2$ by $\rho(R_\theta^k) = R_{k\theta}$.

If $\theta$ is not a multiple of $\pi$, the rotation has no real eigenvectors, so no invariant line. The representation is irreducible over $\mathbb{R}$.

The special case $\theta = \pi$ flips every vector to its negative. Now every line through the origin is invariant — multiplying by $-1$ keeps a line where it was. The representation splits as $\mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}$, with $\mathbb{Z}/2$ acting by $\pm 1$ on each copy.

**Rotation of $\mathbb{R}^3$ by a fixed rotation $R$.** Every $R \in \mathrm{SO}(3)$ has an axis. The axis is a one-dimensional invariant subspace where $G = \langle R \rangle$ acts trivially, and the perpendicular plane is a two-dimensional invariant subspace where $G$ acts by rotation in the plane — exactly the previous example. So

$$
\mathbb{R}^3 = (\text{axis}) \oplus (\text{perpendicular plane})
$$

is the Maschke decomposition. The axis piece is irreducible because every one-dimensional representation is. The plane piece is irreducible iff the angle isn't a multiple of $\pi$.
