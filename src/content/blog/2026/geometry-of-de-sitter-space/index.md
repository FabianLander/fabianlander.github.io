Steve and I spent yesterday at the blackboard working out de Sitter space. The goal is coordinates well-adapted to surfaces of revolution. This post records the steps in order.

## Minkowski space

Throughout, $\mathbb{R}^{n,1}$ denotes $(n+1)$-dimensional **Minkowski space**: the real vector space $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ equipped with the symmetric bilinear form

$$
\langle x, y \rangle = x_1 y_1 + \cdots + x_n y_n - x_{n+1} y_{n+1},
$$

of signature $(n, 1)$. We write $Q(x) = \langle x, x \rangle$ for the associated quadratic form. A nonzero vector $v$ is **spacelike** if $\langle v, v \rangle > 0$, **timelike** if $\langle v, v \rangle < 0$, and **null** if $\langle v, v \rangle = 0$.

## Isometries

An isometry of $\mathbb{R}^{n,1}$ is an affine map that preserves $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$. These form the Poincaré group, generated by translations and the **Lorentz group** $O(n,1)$ of linear maps preserving the form. We will use one family of Lorentz transformations repeatedly: reflections through non-degenerate subspaces. For any linear subspace $W \subset \mathbb{R}^{n,1}$ on which $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is non-degenerate, the form gives an orthogonal decomposition $\mathbb{R}^{n,1} = W \oplus W^{\perp}$, and the **reflection through $W$**

$$
R_W \colon w + w' \mapsto w - w' \qquad (w \in W,\ w' \in W^{\perp})
$$

is a linear isometry. It fixes $W$ pointwise and acts as $-\mathrm{id}$ on $W^{\perp}$.

## de Sitter space

**de Sitter space** is the level set

$$
\mathrm{dS}^n = \{\, x \in \mathbb{R}^{n,1} : \langle x, x \rangle = 1 \,\}.
$$

There is an analog from the Riemannian setting. The three constant-curvature Riemannian model spaces (the sphere, Euclidean space, hyperbolic space) admit similar embedded descriptions inside a flat ambient space. The same construction works in Lorentzian signature, and de Sitter is the Lorentzian analog of the sphere.

Next we check that $\mathrm{dS}^n$ is a Lorentzian manifold: a smooth manifold whose tangent spaces carry a non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form of signature $(n-1, 1)$.

**Smoothness.** The differential of $Q$ is

$$
dQ_x(v) = 2\,\langle x, v \rangle.
$$

For $x \in \mathrm{dS}^n$ we have $\langle x, x \rangle = 1$, so $dQ_x \neq 0$ (it does not vanish on $v = x$ itself). Hence $1$ is a regular value of $Q$, and $\mathrm{dS}^n$ is a smooth codimension-one submanifold of $\mathbb{R}^{n,1}$.

**Tangent spaces.** From the same computation,

$$
T_x \mathrm{dS}^n = \ker dQ_x = \{\, v \in \mathbb{R}^{n,1} : \langle x, v \rangle = 0 \,\} = x^{\perp},
$$

the Minkowski-orthogonal complement of $x$.

**Signature.** Since $\langle x, x \rangle = 1 > 0$, the vector $x$ is spacelike and the form is non-degenerate on $\operatorname{span}(x)$. We get the orthogonal decomposition

$$
\mathbb{R}^{n,1} = \operatorname{span}(x) \oplus x^{\perp}.
$$

The ambient form has signature $(n, 1)$ and its restriction to $\operatorname{span}(x)$ is positive-definite, so the restriction to $x^{\perp}$ has signature $(n-1, 1)$. This makes $\mathrm{dS}^n$ a Lorentzian manifold.

## Geodesics

We construct the geodesics of $\mathrm{dS}^n$ by reflection. Fix a point $p \in \mathrm{dS}^n$ and a non-null tangent vector $v \in T_p \mathrm{dS}^n$, and let

$$
\Pi = \operatorname{span}(p, v) \subset \mathbb{R}^{n,1}.
$$

This is a 2-plane through the origin. In the basis $(p, v)$ the Gram matrix of the Minkowski form on $\Pi$ is diagonal with entries $\langle p, p \rangle = 1$ and $\langle v, v \rangle \neq 0$ (the off-diagonal entry vanishes because $v \in T_p \mathrm{dS}^n = p^{\perp}$). So the form is non-degenerate on $\Pi$, and the reflection $R_\Pi$ from the previous section is defined. As a linear isometry of $\mathbb{R}^{n,1}$ it preserves the level set $\mathrm{dS}^n$.

The fixed-point set of $R_\Pi$ acting on the ambient Minkowski space is $\Pi$ itself. Restricting to $\mathrm{dS}^n$, the fixed-point set of $R_\Pi |_{\mathrm{dS}^n}$ is $\Pi \cap \mathrm{dS}^n$.

Fixed-point sets of isometries are totally geodesic in any (pseudo-)Riemannian manifold; we record a careful proof in the appendix. So $\Pi \cap \mathrm{dS}^n$ is totally geodesic in $\mathrm{dS}^n$.

Now we count dimensions. Write $x \in \Pi$ in the basis $(p, v)$ as $x = ap + bv$. Then $\langle x, x \rangle = a^2 + b^2 \langle v, v \rangle$, so

$$
\Pi \cap \mathrm{dS}^n = \{\, (a, b) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : a^2 + \langle v, v \rangle\, b^2 = 1 \,\}.
$$

This is a conic in $\Pi$: a circle when $v$ is spacelike, a hyperbola (two branches) when $v$ is timelike. Either way it is one-dimensional. The connected component containing $p$ is therefore a geodesic of $\mathrm{dS}^n$ through $p$ with initial velocity a positive multiple of $v$.

## Appendix: fixed-point sets of isometries are totally geodesic

The fact used in the geodesic construction is standard, but worth recording carefully. Let $(M, g)$ be a (pseudo-)Riemannian manifold and $\phi \colon M \to M$ an isometry. Set $F = \mathrm{Fix}(\phi) = \{\, q \in M : \phi(q) = q \,\}$.

**Proposition.** $F$ is a closed embedded submanifold of $M$, totally geodesic, with tangent space
$$
T_q F = \ker\bigl(d\phi_q - \mathrm{id}_{T_q M}\bigr) \quad \text{at each } q \in F.
$$

The proof uses two facts about isometries:

(a) **Naturality of the exponential map.** For $q \in F$ and any $v$ in the domain of $\exp_q$,
$$
\phi(\exp_q v) = \exp_{\phi(q)}(d\phi_q\, v) = \exp_q(d\phi_q\, v).
$$

(b) **Uniqueness of geodesics.** A geodesic in $M$ is uniquely determined by its initial position and velocity (standard ODE).

**Proof.** $F$ is closed because $\phi$ is continuous.

*$F$ is a submanifold near each $q \in F$, with the stated tangent space.* Set $E_+ = \ker(d\phi_q - \mathrm{id}) \subset T_q M$. Choose an open neighborhood $U \subset T_q M$ of $0$ on which $\exp_q$ is a diffeomorphism onto an open $V \subset M$. By continuity of $\phi$ and $\phi(q) = q$, shrink to an open $V' \subset V$ with $q \in V'$ and $\phi(V') \subset V$, and set $U' = \exp_q^{-1}(V') \subset U$.

Claim: $F \cap V' = \exp_q(E_+ \cap U')$.

For $v \in E_+ \cap U'$, fact (a) gives $\phi(\exp_q v) = \exp_q(d\phi_q v) = \exp_q v$, so $\exp_q v \in F \cap V'$.

Conversely, take $q' \in F \cap V'$ and write $q' = \exp_q v$ with $v \in U'$. Then $\phi(q') = q' \in V$, and by (a), $\phi(q') = \exp_q(d\phi_q v)$, so $d\phi_q v$ also lies in $\exp_q^{-1}(V) = U$. Both $v$ and $d\phi_q v$ are in $U$, where $\exp_q$ is injective, so the equality $\exp_q(d\phi_q v) = \exp_q v$ forces $d\phi_q v = v$, i.e., $v \in E_+$.

So $F \cap V'$ is the image under the diffeomorphism $\exp_q|_{U'}$ of the linear subspace $E_+ \cap U'$. Hence $F$ is a smooth embedded submanifold near $q$, with $T_q F = E_+$.

*$F$ is totally geodesic.* Take $q \in F$ and $w \in T_q F = E_+$. Let $\gamma \colon I \to M$ be the maximal geodesic with $\gamma(0) = q$ and $\gamma'(0) = w$. Since $\phi$ is an isometry, $\phi \circ \gamma$ is also a geodesic, with
$$
(\phi \circ \gamma)(0) = \phi(q) = q, \qquad (\phi \circ \gamma)'(0) = d\phi_q(w) = w.
$$
By (b), $\phi \circ \gamma = \gamma$ on $I$. Hence $\gamma(t) \in F$ for all $t \in I$.

This shows that every geodesic of $M$ tangent to $F$ at a point of $F$ stays in $F$, i.e., $F$ is totally geodesic. $\square$

Specialised to our setting: $M = \mathbb{R}^{n,1}$ (or $\mathrm{dS}^n$) and $\phi = R_\Pi$, the reflection through the non-degenerate 2-plane $\Pi$. The fixed set of $R_\Pi$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n,1}$ is $\Pi$, and the fixed set of $R_\Pi |_{\mathrm{dS}^n}$ is $\Pi \cap \mathrm{dS}^n$.
